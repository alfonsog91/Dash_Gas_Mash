    Help: what everything means

    What the percentages mean

    • "X% chance" — Estimated probability of getting a worthwhile order if you wait at this spot for the time you set (the "horizon" slider). Higher = better spot. This is not a guarantee — it's a heuristic guess from public map data.
    • Confidence range (e.g., 25%–45%) — How much the score could realistically swing. A narrow range means the estimate is stable; a wide range means few restaurants back it up, so take it with a grain of salt.
    • Chance of any order — Likelihood of getting any order at all (including bad ones). The main score filters this down to good orders.
    • Avg ticket quality — How pricey nearby restaurants tend to be. Higher-ticket restaurants tend to correlate with better tips.
    • Demand mix — How much of the score comes from nearby merchants versus residential demand anchors. Residential pull is structural context, not a live order feed.
    • Overall strength (Composite) — Blends all four factors (ticket size, restaurant density, proximity, crowding) with weights that shift by time of day.

    The four signal bars

    • Avg ticket size — Are nearby restaurants higher-priced? (Green bar)
    • Restaurant density — How many restaurants are in range? (Blue bar)
    • Proximity — How close is the nearest restaurant? Closer = faster pickup. (Purple bar)
    • Crowding — How many other parking lots are nearby? More parking = more likely other drivers are competing. (Red bar — higher is worse)

    Hold vs Rotate

    ✅ Hold means the spot is strong enough to sit and wait.
    ↻ Rotate means you're probably better off driving to a different area.

    Sliders

    • Local hour: What time of day to model. Late night boosts fast food weight.
    • Probability horizon: "Chance within the next X minutes." Longer = higher %, but that doesn't mean the spot is better — you're just waiting longer.
    • Distance decay: How far restaurants count. Smaller = only very close restaurants matter.
    • Competition strength: Penalizes spots near many parking lots (rough proxy for other drivers). Set to 0 to ignore.
    • Residential demand blend: Adds nearby housing and apartment anchors as a structural demand field. Set to 0 for the pre-promotion merchant-only model.
    • Tip emphasis: Slide right to favor higher-ticket restaurants. Slide left to favor short pickup distance.
    • Grid step: Heatmap detail. Smaller = prettier but slower.
    • K spots / Min separation: How many parking suggestions to show. Min separation applies to MIP mode; the approximate fallback spreads picks using diminishing returns instead of a hard spacing rule.

    Notes & limitations
    • This uses publicly available OSM POIs as a proxy for merchant density and late-night demand.
    • It does not use any proprietary DoorDash dispatch logic, merchant volume, customer demand, or courier supply data.
    • Use this for strategy exploration, not guarantees.
